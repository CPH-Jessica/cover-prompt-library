Your AI Cover Is Legal. Your Disclosure Probably Isn't.

The hybrid-work copyright path the Copyright Office confirmed in 2025, the KDP disclosure rule everyone misses, and what to actually do.

← Back to Library

The workflow most authors are running, the rule we keep getting wrong, and what changed in 2025 that nobody told you.

A TIP student sent me a question this week, and the second I read it I knew it was the question.

She asked: "Is it okay if I generate the image in AI and then use Book Brush or Canva to build the rest of the cover?"

It was the question because I am hearing some version of it from every author I work with right now. Substack DMs. Skool. Discovery calls. The Facebook group. Everybody is using AI for some piece of their covers. Almost nobody actually knows the rules. And the rules changed in 2025 in a way that matters.

So okay, listen. Let me give you the answer fast, then unpack why it is not the answer most people want.

Yes. The workflow works. But not the way most people think it does.

Generating an image in AI — Ideogram, Grok, Gemini, Midjourney, take your pick — and then bringing it into Book Brush or Canva to add typography, layout, series branding, and the actual cover design? That is the smartest workflow you can run right now.

Here is why.

In February 2025, the US Copyright Office published Part 2 of their Copyright and Artificial Intelligence Report. The headline is the part everybody remembers: pure AI output, prompt-only, with no meaningful human authorship — not copyrightable. The Supreme Court declined to hear the Thaler v. Perlmutter appeal in March 2026, which means that ruling is settled law for now.

What people skip past is the rest of the report. The Copyright Office said very clearly that hybrid works ARE registrable when a human did meaningful creative work — selecting, arranging, modifying, compositing, designing around AI-generated material. They proved it the same month by accepting the registration of an artwork called A Single Piece of American Cheese, created with the AI tool Invoke, because the human "selection, arrangement, and coordination" of the AI-generated material was substantial enough to count as authorship.

Translate that to romance covers: AI generates the cover model. You bring it into Book Brush. You add your custom title font. You blend in your series banner. You composite a secondary background. You color-grade. You build the cover around the AI image like the AI image is a stock photo you have license to use.

That cover — the finished piece, your design work plus the AI image — is registrable. The raw AI image alone is not. The wrapper is what makes it yours.

This is honestly a gift. Indie authors have been treating AI covers like a legal grey zone for two years. The Copyright Office just told us the path. Generate the image. Design around it like a real designer would. Register the finished work. Done.

And here is the rule that is going to bite you if you do not pay attention.

The Book Brush + Canva finishing layer makes your cover MORE copyrightable. It does not make KDP's disclosure requirement go away.

This is the part nobody wants to hear. I see authors confidently telling each other in Facebook groups that "I added text in Canva so it is AI-assisted, not AI-generated." Honey, no.

Per KDP's own community guidance — this is from KDP staff, not me, not a third-party blog — the threshold is where the underlying image came from. Not how much design work you did on top.

  • AI-Generated: The image was created by an AI tool. Even if you spent hours editing, even if you put it through three rounds of Canva, even if you added typography and series branding. Original image came from AI → AI-generated → disclose on KDP.
  • AI-Assisted: YOU created the original (you painted it, photographed it, commissioned it from a human artist), and AI only refined it (sharpening, color correction, light cleanup). No disclosure required.

If your base image came from Midjourney, Ideogram, Grok, Gemini, DALL-E, or anything similar — you check the disclosure box. Even if Book Brush touched it. Even if Canva did the typography. Even if you art-directed the hell out of it after the fact.

The good news, which I said in the community post and will say again because authors keep missing it: the KDP disclosure is confidential. It does not show up to readers. It does not appear on your product page. It does not hurt your approval rate. It is a checkbox in your KDP dashboard that nobody but Amazon ever sees.

The thing that gets books pulled is failing to check it. Not checking it.

And one more thing about IngramSpark, because this one is uglier.

Amazon KDP is the polite version. IngramSpark is harsher.

IngramSpark's Catalog Integrity Guidelines prohibit "content created using automated means, including but not limited to content generated using artificial intelligence." In practice, they treat your book as one binary: is it AI-generated, or is it not.

There is no "AI cover only" option. If your cover came from AI, you have to declare the whole book partially AI-generated when you upload to IngramSpark. The manuscript can be 100% you. The cover image is AI. The whole title gets the partial-AI flag.

If you use IngramSpark for paperback distribution, this is a real consideration. It is not a legal landmine — they accept books — but it is a different posture than KDP, and you should know going in.

The thing nobody tells you about prompting for cover models.

Real talk for a second. I run eleven pen names. Across four genres. With a husband who writes psychological thriller. The volume of cover work in this business is brutal, and AI has been a meaningful unlock — including for books I have built that I could not have afforded to design otherwise.

But there is one rule I will go to the mat on, and it is this: do not prompt for the names of real people. Not actors. Not athletes. Not BookTok cover models. Not anyone identifiable.

This is not me being cautious for the sake of it. This is right of publicity, and every single person has it — not just celebrities. Generating a cover model that "happens to look exactly like" a known person is the same legal risk as photographing them and slapping their face on your book. The fact that AI did the photography does not protect you. You published it. You are responsible.

Several states are passing laws that target AI likenesses specifically. Tennessee's ELVIS Act. Similar in California and Illinois. The legal landscape on this is hardening, fast. Your AI cover model needs to look like a person who does not exist. That is the bar.

So here is the workflow, end-to-end.

Because I know you came here for the actual workflow.

You generate the base image in whichever AI tool fits the aesthetic — Ideogram if you need clean text on the image, Midjourney or Grok for the lush atmospheric romance and fantasy stuff (as of May 2026 — these tools are moving fast and this is going to read differently in six months), Gemini Imagen for cleaner photorealistic composition, Adobe Firefly if you want the lowest legal exposure because they are the only one with IP indemnification baked in.

You save the prompt. You save the date. This is your Creative Audit Trail — proof of what you generated and when.

You bring the image into Book Brush or Canva. You design the actual cover. Typography. Layout. Series branding. Compositing. Color grading. Whatever the cover needs.

You document the design work. A screenshot of your layers, a saved version of your Book Brush project, anything that shows the human design judgment you applied.

If the cover matters enough — a major release, BELOW for me, anything you would hate to see ripped off — you register the finished cover with the US Copyright Office. The hybrid work is registrable.

You upload to KDP. You check the AI-generated disclosure box. It is confidential. Move on.

If you are also going wide via IngramSpark, you flag your title as partially AI-generated there too.

That is the entire workflow. Not complicated. Just specific.

Because I have a cover design class coming, and as I built the curriculum I realized half of what I needed to teach was not actually about design — it was about which rules apply where, and how to make smart calls instead of guessing.

Indie author opinions about AI covers are loud and often wrong. Some people will tell you AI covers are stealing. Some people will tell you the rules do not apply to them. Both groups are confidently incorrect.

The actual rules — Copyright Office, KDP, IngramSpark, right-of-publicity laws — are knowable. They are just not in any one place. So I am putting them in one place.

If you are using AI for any part of your covers, you do not need permission to keep doing it. You need to know where the lines are so you can publish with confidence instead of waiting to get a takedown.
