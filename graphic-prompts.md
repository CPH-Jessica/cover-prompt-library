**Notes before you generate:**

- Aspect ratios suggested for each, but adjust to your platform.
- Color palette across all of them: warm purples, dusty rose, soft cream, deep plum (TIP-ish, professional women over 40 audience). Adjust if your branding has shifted.

## QUICK TIPS FOR PROMPTING

A few things I learned that apply to both Gemini and Grok specifically:

**Gemini / Imagen** tends to be more literal with composition instructions. The phrases "flat-lay overhead view," "left side / right side," and "center of the image" do real work. It also tends to render text accidentally if you don't explicitly say "no text in the image" — which is in every prompt above.

**Grok / Aurora** is more painterly and atmospheric by default. It will lean toward romance/fantasy moods. Good for the dreamier prompts (#1, #3, #5, #6). Less precise on rigid composition than Gemini.

**For all of these:** if the first generation comes out with a human figure or readable text where you didn't want one, regenerate with the explicit instruction added to the FRONT of the prompt: *"No human figures or faces. No readable text or letters."* That phrasing at the start tends to be more reliable than at the end.

**If you want to add YOUR title text or a quote in Canva / Book Brush after generation** — that's the recommended workflow for any of the quote cards. The AI is generating the background; you add the type. That's also exactly the AI-image + Book Brush/Canva workflow you're writing about, so eat your own cooking.

## QUICK NOTE ON BRAND CONSISTENCY

All seven prompts in Part 2 use the same core color palette family — deep plum, warm cream, antique gold, dusty rose — so they read as a coordinated set when subscribers see them in sequence (article hero → paid library hero → PDF cover → chat announcement).

If your TIP brand has shifted away from this palette since you set up Substack, swap the color names but keep the structural logic: warm + sophisticated + one accent metallic. That combination is what reads as "premium resource."

If you want to A/B test, generate options #11 and #12 both and see which one resonates more in your audience. The velvet/vintage feels more romance-author-coded; the marble/minimalist feels more modern-creative-coded. Both work — depends which version of TIP you're leaning into for 2026.
